The History of Cookies

Origins

The history of the cookie dates back to 7th century Persia (modern-day Iran), where sugar, a crucial ingredient in cookies, was first cultivated. With the spread of sugar through trade routes, baking techniques and recipes began to disseminate across the known world.

Medieval Europe

By the Middle Ages, cookies had spread to Europe, becoming a common treat among the wealthy. Early European cookies were often spiced and sweetened with honey. Dutch settlers in America are credited with bringing the concept of the cookie to the New World. In fact, the word "cookie" is derived from the Dutch word "koekje," meaning "little cake."

17th and 18th Centuries

During the 17th and 18th centuries, cookies became popular throughout Europe. Different regions developed their own unique varieties, often influenced by local ingredients and culinary traditions. For example, shortbread from Scotland and macarons from France emerged as iconic cookies.

The 19th Century

The Industrial Revolution in the 19th century significantly impacted cookie production. Advances in technology allowed for mass production of baked goods, making cookies more widely available and affordable. This era also saw the creation of some classic cookie recipes that are still beloved today, such as the gingerbread and sugar cookies.

The 20th Century and Beyond

The 20th century marked the rise of the cookie in popular culture, especially in the United States. The invention of chocolate chip cookies by Ruth Wakefield in 1938 revolutionized the cookie industry. Wakefield, owner of the Toll House Inn, accidentally created the cookies by adding broken chocolate bar pieces into her dough, leading to the birth of the now-iconic chocolate chip cookie.

Modern Day

Today, cookies come in an endless variety of flavors, shapes, and sizes. They are enjoyed around the world, both as a simple homemade treat and a commercial product. The rise of the internet has even led to a global exchange of recipes and innovations, further diversifying and enriching cookie traditions. Cookies have evolved from simple, sweet biscuits to complex, gourmet confections. Their history reflects broader trends in culinary history, trade, and technological advancement, making them a fascinating subject of study for food historians and enthusiasts alike.
